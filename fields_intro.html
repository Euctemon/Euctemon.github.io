Introduction

Fields are an important object in mathematics. In abstract algebra one can study various connections between groups and fields which is for example helpful for reasoning about the possibility to find roots of a polynomial. More down to earth usage of fields is when one needs to solve a system of linear algebraic equations. The coefficients by the unknowns must be from a field. Why is that? Let's look at an example

$$\begin{aligned} 3x+2y&=1 \\ x-y&=2 \\ \end{aligned}$$

What do we do when we try to solve this system of equations? First we move the \(y\) to the other side of the equation with an opposite sign to get \(x=2+y\). We substitute this expression to the first equation to obtain \(3(2+y)+2y=1\). We multiply the content in the brackets to get \((6+3y)+2y=1\). We move the brackets to obtain \(6+(3y+2y)\) and add the coefficients for the variable \(y\) to get \(6+5y=1\). We subtract the number 6 from both sides and divide by the number \(5\) to find out that \(y=-1\). We substitute the result to the second equation to get \(x=1\). We used a lot of properties of numbers to arrive at this result. We freely used associativity of adding, the distributive property, existence of additive and multiplicative inverses and so on. How does a formal definition of fields look like? It is not pretty as a field is quite a complex algebraic structure. A lot of us needed to learn the propertis like associativity or distributivity of rationals or real numbers during math classes in highschool. Here is the definition

Field is a set \(R\) with two binary operations \(\odot : R \rightarrow R \rightarrow R\) and \(\oplus : R \rightarrow R \rightarrow R\) and two distinct elements \(1 \in R\) and \(0 \in R\) such that they satisfy following axioms

  • \(\forall \, a,b,c \in R\) it follows that \((a \oplus b) \oplus c = a \oplus (b \oplus c)\)
  • \(\forall \, a \in R\) it follows that \(a \oplus 0 = a\)
  • \(\forall \, a \in R \enspace \exists \, b \in R\) such that \(a \oplus b = 0\)
  • \(\forall \, a,b \in R\) it follows that \(a \oplus b = b \oplus a\)
  • \(\forall \, a,b,c \in R\) it follows that \((a \odot b) \odot c = a \odot (b \odot c)\)
  • \(\forall \, a \in R\) it follows that \(a \odot 1 = a\)
  • \(\forall \, a \in R \enspace \exists \, b \in R\) such that \(a \odot b = 0\)
  • \(\forall \, a,b \in R\) it follows that \(a \odot b = b \odot a\)
  • \(\forall \, a,b,c \in R\) it follows that \(a \odot (b \oplus c) = a \odot b \oplus a \odot c\)

This is a long list of properties and it might be hard to remember them if one does not use them often.
